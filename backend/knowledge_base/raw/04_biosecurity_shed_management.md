# Biosecurity and Shed Management — Bangladesh Poultry Farms

## What is Biosecurity?

Biosecurity means all measures taken to prevent diseases from entering and spreading on a poultry farm. It has three core components:
1. **Isolation** — keeping your flock separate from potential disease sources
2. **Traffic Control** — managing movement of people, vehicles, and equipment
3. **Sanitation** — cleaning and disinfection

### Current Situation in Bangladesh
- Most commercial farms in Bangladesh have inadequate biosecurity measures
- Backyard and Sonali farms rarely practice standard biosecurity
- Poor biosecurity is a major factor in disease outbreaks (Newcastle, AI, Gumboro)

## Shed Design and Construction

### Orientation
- Orient sheds **east-west** (lengthwise) to minimize direct sunlight
- In Bangladesh, prevailing wind is from south/southwest — position open sides to catch breeze

### Floor Space Requirements

| Bird Type | Floor Space (sq ft per bird) |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Broiler (deep litter) | 1.0-1.2 |
| Layer (deep litter) | 2.0-2.5 |
| Layer (cage) | 0.5-0.7 (per cage space) |
| Sonali (semi-intensive) | 1.5-2.0 |
| Chick (0-4 weeks) | 0.5 |
| Grower (4-8 weeks) | 0.75-1.0 |

### Ventilation
- **Critical in Bangladesh's hot, humid climate**
- Open-sided sheds are most common (suitable for most of Bangladesh)
- Minimum shed width: 25-30 feet for natural ventilation
- Eave height: 8-10 feet
- Ridge height: 12-14 feet
- Ridge ventilation opening: 6-12 inches (helps hot air escape)
- Use wire mesh (1-inch) on open sides to prevent wild bird entry
- In environmentally controlled (EC) houses: maintain negative pressure ventilation

### Roof
- Use thatch, tile, or corrugated metal sheet
- **Thatching with paddy straw reduces roof temperature significantly**
- Paint metal roof white (reflects sunlight)
- Insulate underside of metal roof with polystyrene or rice husk bags
- Roof overhang: at least 3-4 feet to prevent rain entry

### Curtains
- Use curtain system (top-down) for rain and wind protection
- Curtain material: woven polypropylene or jute
- Open curtains from bottom in hot weather for cross-ventilation
- Close from top during cold weather and storms

## Litter Management

### Litter Materials Used in Bangladesh
| Material | Availability | Cost | Quality |
|----------|-------------|------|---------|
| Rice husk (তুষ) | Very common | Low | Good — most recommended |
| Sawdust (কাঠের গুঁড়া) | Common | Low-Medium | Good, but can be dusty |
| Wood shavings | Less common | Medium | Good |
| Chopped straw | Available | Low | Fair — tends to mat |

### Litter Depth
- **Broiler**: 2-3 inches (5-8 cm)
- **Layer (deep litter)**: 4-6 inches (10-15 cm)
- In hot weather: reduce litter to 1.5-2 inches (400-450 grams per sq ft)

### Litter Moisture Management
- Target litter moisture: **20-30%**
- Moisture above 30% leads to:
  - Ammonia buildup
  - Coccidiosis and bacterial proliferation
  - Foot pad dermatitis (পায়ের তলায় ঘা)
  - Breast blisters
- **Monitor daily**: pick up a handful — it should crumble, not stick together
- Stir litter daily or every other day
- Fix leaking drinkers immediately (main cause of wet litter)
- In monsoon: increase ventilation, use lime (1kg per 10 sq ft) under litter if floor is damp

### Ammonia Control
- Ammonia should be **below 25 ppm** (you should not smell it at bird level)
- If you can smell ammonia strongly, levels are dangerously high (>50 ppm)
- High ammonia causes:
  - Respiratory damage
  - Eye irritation (keratoconjunctivitis)
  - Reduced growth and feed intake
  - Increased susceptibility to Newcastle Disease
- **Solutions**: improve ventilation, stir litter, fix wet areas, use acidifying agents (alum, superphosphate)

### Litter Reuse
- In Bangladesh, broiler farmers often reuse rice husk litter for 2-3 flocks to save cost
- **Risks**: increased disease, ammonia, and pathogen load
- If reusing: remove caked litter, add fresh rice husk on top, treat with lime
- **Best practice**: full litter replacement and disinfection between each flock

## Cleaning and Disinfection Between Flocks

### Step-by-Step Protocol

1. **Remove all birds** — sell or transfer entire flock
2. **Remove all equipment** — feeders, drinkers, curtains
3. **Remove all litter** — sweep completely
4. **Dry cleaning** — sweep/scrape walls, floors, ceiling
5. **Wash equipment** — soap and water, then sun-dry
6. **Wet wash** — pressure wash entire shed with water and detergent
7. **Disinfect** — spray all surfaces with disinfectant:
   - Formalin (2-3%): effective but toxic — wear mask and gloves
   - Quaternary ammonium compounds (QAC): safer, effective at 0.1-0.2%
   - Sodium hypochlorite (bleach): 0.5% solution
   - Glutaraldehyde: broad-spectrum, 2% solution
8. **Fumigation** (optional but recommended):
   - Close all openings
   - Place formalin (40ml) + potassium permanganate (20g) per 100 cubic feet
   - Leave sealed for 24 hours
   - Open and ventilate for 48 hours before placing birds
   - **Caution**: Formalin fumes are toxic — do NOT enter during fumigation
9. **Lime wash** — apply whitewash (quicklime slurry) on floor and lower walls
10. **Rest period** — keep shed empty for **14-21 days** minimum
11. **Spread new litter** — fresh, dry rice husk
12. **Pre-heat shed** (for chicks) 24-48 hours before placement

## Visitor and Traffic Control

### Rules for Farm Entry
- Restrict entry to essential personnel only
- All visitors must:
  - Sign visitor log (date, name, purpose, last farm visited)
  - Wash hands with soap
  - Dip footwear in footbath (2-3% formalin or QAC)
  - Wear farm-provided boots/overalls (if available)
- Do not allow:
  - Feed delivery trucks inside the farm compound
  - Visitors who have been to another poultry farm within 48 hours
  - Entry of dogs, cats, or other animals
- Arrange feed/supply delivery at farm gate, not inside
- Dead bird disposal area should be away from sheds and not accessible to stray animals

### Vehicle Sanitation
- Spray vehicle tires and undercarriage with disinfectant at farm entrance
- Vehicles transporting live birds should be disinfected after each trip
- Keep separate vehicle for feed delivery and bird transport

## Rodent and Pest Control

### Common Pests in Bangladesh Poultry Farms
- Rats and mice (ইঁদুর) — eat feed, spread disease, damage equipment
- Wild birds (কাক, চড়ুই) — major carriers of Avian Influenza and Newcastle
- Flies (মাছি) — breed in manure, spread bacteria
- Darkling beetles (lesser mealworm) — harbor Salmonella, E. coli in litter
- Mosquitoes (মশা) — transmit Fowl Pox

### Control Methods
- **Rats**: 
  - Keep area around shed clear (no debris, tall grass, or stored equipment)
  - Use bait stations with anticoagulant rodenticide (bromadiolone)
  - Block entry holes in shed walls (rats can squeeze through 12mm gap)
  - Keep feed store sealed
  - Traps around shed perimeter
- **Wild birds**:
  - Install wire mesh (1-inch/25mm) on all shed openings
  - Repair any holes in mesh promptly
  - Do not leave spilled feed outside shed
  - Do not raise ducks/geese near chicken sheds
- **Flies**:
  - Manage manure — remove or dry promptly
  - Use fly traps around shed
  - Apply larvicide to manure pits
  - Maintain clean surroundings
- **Mosquitoes**:
  - Drain stagnant water within 200m of farm
  - Use mosquito nets on shed openings (during monsoon)
  - Apply mosquito repellent sprays

## Dead Bird Disposal

### Recommended Methods
1. **Burial**: Dig pit at least 3 feet deep, away from water sources. Cover with quicklime and soil.
2. **Composting**: Layer dead birds with litter and rice husk in a compost bin. Temperature reaches 60°C, killing pathogens. Complete in 30-45 days.
3. **Incineration**: For small numbers. Build simple incinerator with brick.

### Do NOT:
- Throw dead birds in open areas, ponds, or rivers
- Feed dead birds to dogs or other animals
- Sell dead or sick birds in the market
- Leave dead birds inside the shed (remove within 1 hour)

## Record Keeping

### Essential Farm Records
Every poultry farmer should maintain:

| Record | Details | Frequency |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Mortality | Number of dead birds per day, cause if known | Daily |
| Feed consumption | Total feed consumed per day | Daily |
| Water consumption | Total water consumed per day | Daily |
| Egg production | Total eggs, broken eggs, sold eggs | Daily (layers) |
| Body weight | Sample 20-30 birds, calculate average | Weekly (broilers) |
| Vaccination | Date, vaccine name, batch number, route | Each event |
| Medication | Drug name, dose, start/end date, withdrawal period | Each event |
| Temperature | Max/min temperature inside shed | Daily |
| Visitors | Name, date, purpose, last farm visit | Each visit |
| Purchases/Sales | Feed, chick, egg, bird sales with amounts | Each transaction |

### Why Records Matter
- Detect problems early (mortality spike, feed intake drop)
- Track performance against targets
- Calculate profitability
- Provide evidence for insurance claims
- Help veterinarian diagnose problems
- Required for DLS certification
