# Heat Stress and Weather Management for Bangladesh Poultry

## Bangladesh Climate and Poultry

Bangladesh has a subtropical monsoon climate with distinct seasons that directly impact poultry health and production:

| Season | Months | Temperature Range | Key Challenges |
|--------|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Winter (শীত) | November-February | 10-25°C | Cold stress for chicks, respiratory diseases (CRD) |
| Summer (গ্রীষ্ম) | March-May | 30-42°C | **Severe heat stress**, mortality, production drop |
| Monsoon (বর্ষা) | June-September | 27-35°C | High humidity, litter moisture, coccidiosis, fowl pox |
| Autumn (শরৎ) | October-November | 25-32°C | Transition period, generally favorable |

### Comfort Zone for Poultry
- **Ideal temperature**: 22-28°C
- Above 28°C: feed intake starts declining
- Above 32°C: significant heat stress
- Above 35°C: **dangerous — mortality risk**
- Above 40°C: **critical — mass mortality possible within hours**

## Heat Stress — The #1 Weather Problem in Bangladesh

### What Happens During Heat Stress
1. Birds cannot sweat — they cool themselves by panting (rapid breathing)
2. Panting increases respiratory rate from 25 to >250 breaths/minute
3. This causes respiratory alkalosis (CO₂ loss → blood pH rises)
4. Blood flow shifts from organs to skin for cooling
5. Feed intake drops 1.5% for every 1°C above 32°C
6. Egg production drops, shell quality deteriorates
7. If body temperature exceeds 47°C, death occurs

### Signs of Heat Stress
- Open-mouth breathing / panting (মুখ খুলে হাঁপানি)
- Wings held away from body (spreading wings)
- Increased water consumption
- Decreased feed intake
- Pale combs and wattles
- Lethargy, lying down
- Watery droppings
- Drop in egg production
- Thin/soft eggshells
- Reduced egg size
- In severe cases: convulsions and death

## Heat Stress Management — Practical Steps

### Water Management (Most Critical)
- **Increase water access**: Add extra drinkers (1 bell drinker per 75 birds, or 1 nipple per 10-12 birds)
- **Cool water**: Water temperature below 25°C. Add ice if available.
- **Electrolytes in water**: Add electrolyte + Vitamin C supplement
  - Formula: 1.5g Vitamin C + 1g KCl + 0.5g NaHCO₃ per liter of water
  - Or use commercial electrolyte powder as per label
- **Never let water run out** — even 30 minutes without water in extreme heat can cause mortality
- Clean water lines daily in summer (biofilm builds faster in heat)
- Water consumption increases 2-3× in hot weather

### Feeding Management
- **Feed during cool hours**: Early morning (4-6 AM) and evening (6-8 PM)
- **Withdraw feed during peak heat** (12 PM - 4 PM) — empty gut generates less metabolic heat
- **Increase energy density**: Add 2-3% vegetable oil to feed (fat generates less metabolic heat than carbohydrates)
- **Maintain feed protein quality** but don't increase crude protein (excess protein generates heat during digestion)
- **Add sodium bicarbonate (baking soda)**: 2-3g per kg of feed — helps counter alkalosis from panting
- **Vitamin C**: 200-300mg per liter of water — most important heat stress supplement
- **Vitamin E**: 250mg per kg of feed — antioxidant protection
- Use pellet feed (requires less metabolic energy to digest than mash)

### Ventilation and Cooling
- **Open all curtains** from the bottom during heat
- **Use fans**: ceiling fans or exhaust fans to increase air movement
  - Air speed of 2-3 m/s at bird level provides significant cooling
- **Foggers/misting systems**: Can reduce temperature by 5-10°C
  - Use in sheds with good ventilation (otherwise increases humidity dangerously)
  - Operate when humidity is below 70%
- **Sprinkle water on roof**: Reduces shed temperature by 3-5°C
- **Wet gunny bags on open sides**: Creates evaporative cooling effect
- **Plant trees around shed**: Provides shade, reduces ambient temperature by 2-3°C
  - Maintain 10m distance from shed walls for biosecurity

### Shed Modifications for Summer
- Paint roof white or use reflective material
- Thatch roof with paddy straw or grass (excellent insulation)
- Increase roof height if possible
- Install ridge ventilators
- Extend roof overhang to 4-5 feet
- Reduce stocking density by 20-30% in summer:
  - Broilers: 1.2-1.5 sq ft per bird (instead of 1.0)
  - Layers: 2.5-3.0 sq ft per bird (instead of 2.0)

### Lighting Management
- Avoid additional lighting during peak heat hours
- For layers: provide light early morning (3-4 AM start) to encourage feeding in cool hours
- For broilers: consider intermittent lighting programs to reduce activity during heat

### Emergency Response (Extreme Heat >40°C)
1. **Immediately**: Ensure unlimited cool water
2. Add Vitamin C + electrolytes to water
3. Increase ventilation to maximum (all fans, open all sides)
4. Start misting/fogging if available
5. Sprinkle water on roof and floor
6. Remove dead birds promptly
7. Do NOT handle or vaccinate birds during extreme heat
8. If mortality exceeds 0.5% in one hour, consider spraying birds with water mist
9. Contact veterinarian if mortality continues

## Cold Stress Management (Winter: November-February)

### Risk Factors
- Night temperatures in northern Bangladesh can drop to 5-8°C
- Chicks are most vulnerable (require 32-35°C in first week)
- Cold drafts cause respiratory diseases

### Management Practices
- **Brooding**: Maintain temperature schedule:

| Chick Age | Temperature (°C) |
|-----------|------------------|
| Day 1-3 | 33-35 |
| Day 4-7 | 31-33 |
| Week 2 | 28-30 |
| Week 3 | 25-28 |
| Week 4 | 22-25 |
| Week 5+ | 20-22 (ambient) |

- Use brooders (gas/electric/charcoal): 1 brooder per 500-700 chicks
- Close curtains at night (lower from top)
- Use curtain system: open during day, close at night
- Avoid drafts — seal gaps in walls and floor
- Increase litter depth to 4-6 inches for insulation
- Maintain dry litter (damp litter + cold = high CRD risk)
- Use warm water in very cold mornings
- Increase feed by 5-10% (birds need more energy to maintain body temperature)

## Monsoon Management (June-September)

### Key Challenges
- High humidity (80-100%) makes litter wet
- Flooding can contaminate water supply
- Mosquito population explodes → Fowl Pox risk
- Feed storage: mold and aflatoxin risk increases
- Waterlogging around sheds

### Management Practices
- **Litter**: Stir daily, add dry rice husk, use lime (CaO) if floor is damp
- **Drainage**: Ensure proper drainage around shed — dig channels, grade ground away from shed
- **Roof leaks**: Repair before monsoon starts
- **Mosquito control**: Drain stagnant water, use mosquito nets on shed openings
- **Fowl Pox vaccination**: Complete before monsoon season (April-May)
- **Feed storage**: Keep bags on pallets, use toxin binders, buy smaller quantities more frequently
- **Water treatment**: Extra vigilance on chlorination — floodwater can contaminate wells
- **Raise shed floor**: If in flood-prone area, raise floor 1-2 feet above ground level
- **Coccidiosis prevention**: Add anticoccidial in feed during monsoon; watch for bloody droppings

## Cyclone and Flood Preparedness

### Bangladesh is highly vulnerable to cyclones and flooding

### Before Cyclone Season (April-May, October-November)
- Strengthen shed structure — reinforce roof and walls
- Trim nearby trees (branches can fall on shed)
- Stock emergency supplies: feed (1 week), medicines, generator fuel
- Identify evacuation plan for birds if shed is in flood-prone area
- Ensure insurance coverage is active

### During a Cyclone/Flood
- Close all curtains and secure them
- Move birds to higher ground if possible
- Ensure continuous power for ventilation (generator backup)
- Do not leave farm unattended
- After the event: check for structural damage before reopening shed

### After Cyclone/Flood
- Assess bird losses and structural damage
- Test water supply for contamination before using
- Clean and disinfect if floodwater entered shed
- Inspect and discard wet/moldy feed
- Watch for disease outbreaks 7-14 days after the event
- Contact Upazila Livestock Office for emergency assistance
- Report losses for government/NGO relief programs
