# Poultry Emergency & First Aid Guide — Bangladesh

## Emergency Decision Matrix

### When to Contact Upazila Livestock Office IMMEDIATELY

| Situation | Urgency | Action |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Sudden death of >5% flock in 24 hours | 🔴 CRITICAL | Report immediately. Suspect AI/ND. Do NOT sell birds. |
| Suspected Avian Influenza (purple combs, sudden mass death) | 🔴 CRITICAL | Report within 24 hours. Government notifiable disease. |
| Neurological signs (neck twisting) in multiple birds | 🔴 CRITICAL | Isolate flock. Suspect Newcastle Disease. |
| Respiratory distress spreading rapidly through flock | 🟡 URGENT | Isolate sick birds. Contact vet within 24 hours. |
| Bloody diarrhea in >10% of flock | 🟡 URGENT | Start anticoccidial treatment. Contact vet. |
| Sudden egg production drop >15% | 🟡 URGENT | Investigate disease. Contact vet if cause unclear. |
| Heat stress mortality starting | 🔴 CRITICAL | Emergency cooling measures immediately. |

## First Aid for Common Emergencies

### 1. Heat Stroke / Mass Heat Stress

**Signs**: Birds lying on side, panting heavily, convulsions, sudden death during hot afternoon.

**Immediate actions (first 30 minutes)**:
1. Move affected birds to shade/cooler area
2. Spray light mist of cool (NOT cold) water over birds
3. Provide electrolyte water (1 tsp salt + 1 tbsp sugar + ½ tsp baking soda per liter)
4. Turn on all fans, open all curtains
5. Sprinkle water on roof
6. Do NOT handle birds roughly — stress increases body temperature
7. Do NOT give ice-cold water (thermal shock)

**Follow-up**:
- Add Vitamin C (300mg/liter) to water for next 3-5 days
- Feed only during cool hours for the next week
- Reduce stocking density if overcrowded
- Repair/improve ventilation system

### 2. Poisoning / Suspected Toxicity

**Signs**: Sudden death in healthy-looking birds, drooling, difficulty breathing, blue/purple comb, diarrhea, tremors.

**Possible sources in Bangladesh**:
- Pesticide-contaminated grain (common in rice-growing areas)
- Aflatoxin in moldy feed (especially during monsoon)
- Rat poison ingestion
- Salt poisoning (from excess salt in feed/water)
- Ammonia toxicity (from poor ventilation)

**Immediate actions**:
1. Remove suspected feed/water source immediately
2. Provide fresh, clean water with activated charcoal (5g per liter) if available
3. Vitamin E + selenium injection if available (supports detoxification)
4. Send feed sample and dead bird to nearest Disease Investigation Laboratory
5. Do NOT sell any birds or eggs until cleared

### 3. Predator Attack

**Common predators in Bangladesh**: Jackals (শেয়াল), stray dogs (কুকুর), cats, mongoose (বেজি), snakes (সাপ), crows (কাক), kites (চিল)

**Immediate actions**:
1. Secure remaining birds in shed
2. Treat injured birds: clean wounds with dilute povidone-iodine (Betadine)
3. Apply antibiotic wound powder
4. Isolate injured birds from flock (pecking risk)
5. Repair shed security (mesh, door, gaps in walls)
6. For snake entry: seal all gaps >1cm, clear vegetation around shed

### 4. Respiratory Emergency

**Signs**: Gasping, mouth breathing, blue/purple comb, rattling sound, nasal discharge in many birds simultaneously.

**Immediate actions**:
1. Improve ventilation immediately (open curtains, turn on fans)
2. Check ammonia level — can you smell it strongly? If yes, stir litter, add dry material
3. Start electrolytes + Vitamin C in water
4. If CRD suspected: begin Tylosin (0.5g/liter) or Doxycycline (10-20mg/kg)
5. If Newcastle suspected: isolate flock, do NOT treat with antibiotics alone, contact vet
6. Keep birds warm if cold weather is a factor
7. Reduce dust: mist shed lightly, avoid dry sweeping

### 5. Leg Problems / Lameness

**Causes**:
- Nutritional: Vitamin D3 / calcium deficiency (rickets in young, cage layer fatigue in hens)
- Infectious: Mycoplasma synoviae (swollen joints), Marek's disease (paralysis)
- Mechanical: Slippery floors, high perches, injury
- Metabolic: Fatty liver (overweight layers)

**Immediate actions**:
1. Isolate lame birds — they will be trampled and bullied
2. Provide water and feed within reach
3. If joints are swollen and hot: suspect bacterial infection → Amoxicillin 20mg/kg for 5 days
4. If one leg forward/backward (splits): suspect Marek's disease → no treatment, cull humanely
5. If legs bent/bowed in chicks: suspect rickets → Vitamin D3 + calcium supplement
6. For cage layer fatigue: provide calcium immediately (crushed oyster shell)

### 6. Egg Binding (Egg Stuck in Oviduct)

**Signs**: Hen straining, sitting on nest for hours, swollen abdomen, walking like a penguin, may become lethargic.

**Immediate actions**:
1. Keep bird calm in a warm, quiet, dark place
2. Apply warm, moist cloth to vent area
3. Apply lubricant (vegetable oil or glycerin) around vent
4. Provide calcium immediately (dissolve calcium tablet in water)
5. Gentle abdominal massage (very careful — egg can break inside)
6. If egg doesn't pass within 4-6 hours, seek veterinary help
7. Do NOT try to pull the egg out — this can cause internal injury

### 7. Prolapse (Vent Blowout)

**Signs**: Red tissue protruding from vent, common in young layers or overweight birds.

**Immediate actions**:
1. Isolate bird immediately (other birds will peck the prolapse — cannibalism)
2. Clean prolapsed tissue with warm saline solution
3. Apply hemorrhoid cream or sugar (reduces swelling through osmosis)
4. Gently push tissue back in (wear clean gloves)
5. Reduce light intensity to decrease laying urge
6. May recur — monitor closely for 48 hours
7. If it recurs repeatedly, culling may be necessary

### 8. Cannibalism / Feather Pecking

**Signs**: Birds pecking at each other, blood on feathers, bare patches, wounds, birds piling on injured flock mate.

**Immediate actions**:
1. Remove injured birds immediately (blood attracts more pecking)
2. Treat wounds with antiseptic + anti-peck spray (purple spray/gentian violet)
3. Reduce light intensity to 5-10 lux
4. Change light color to red if possible (reduces visual stimulation)
5. Check and correct: protein level (especially methionine), overcrowding, boredom
6. Provide enrichment: hanging vegetables (cabbage), scratch grain on litter
7. Ensure adequate feeder/drinker space

## Emergency Supply Kit

Every poultry farm should have these items ready:

### Medicines
| Item | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Electrolyte powder | Dehydration, heat stress, diarrhea |
| Vitamin C powder | Heat stress |
| Vitamin AD₃E | General immune support |

| Amprolium | Coccidiosis |
| Tylosin | CRD/Mycoplasmosis |

| Gentian violet (purple spray) | Anti-peck, wound protection |

|------|---------|

| Weighing scale | Body weight monitoring |
| Record book | Daily records |
| Phone numbers | Vet, Upazila office, feed supplier |

## Dead Bird Examination (Basic Post-Mortem)

**When a bird dies unexpectedly, a basic external and internal examination can help identify the cause.**

### External Examination
| Observation | Possible Cause |
|-------------|---------------|
| Blue/purple comb | Avian Influenza, heart failure, suffocation |
| Swollen head/face | Coryza, AI, cholera |
| Nasal discharge | CRD, IB, ND |
| Bloody vent | Coccidiosis |
| Skin tumors/nodules | Marek's, Fowl Pox |
| Swollen joints | M. synoviae, bacterial arthritis |
| Paralyzed legs (one forward/backward) | Marek's disease |
| Twisted neck | Newcastle Disease |
| Wart-like lesions on comb | Fowl Pox |

### Internal Examination (Open the bird)
| Observation | Possible Cause |
|-------------|---------------|
| Hemorrhages in intestine | Newcastle Disease, Coccidiosis |
| Bloody cecum (blind guts) | Cecal Coccidiosis (E. tenella) |
| Enlarged, mottled liver | Salmonellosis, Cholera |
| Cheesy material in air sacs | Aspergillosis, CRD |
| Enlarged bursa (young birds) | Gumboro |
| Tumors in liver/kidney/spleen | Marek's, Lymphoid Leukosis |
| Fluid around heart | Colibacillosis |
| Egg yolk in abdomen | Egg peritonitis |
| Greenish liver | Aflatoxicosis |

**⚠️ Always wear gloves. Wash hands with soap afterwards. If AI is suspected, do NOT open the bird — send it to the lab.**

## Emergency Contact Framework

### Who to Contact and When

| Level | Contact | When |
|-------|---------|------|
| **Level 1 — Farm level** | Farm manager / experienced worker | First response for any issue |
| **Level 2 — Local vet** | Private veterinarian / para-vet | When farm-level treatment doesn't work in 24 hours |
| **Level 3 — Upazila Livestock Office** | Government veterinary officer | Suspected notifiable disease, mass mortality |
| **Level 4 — District Disease Lab** | Regional FDI Laboratory | Lab diagnosis needed, sample submission |
| **Level 5 — National** | DLS Headquarters, Dhaka | Major outbreak, AI confirmation |

### Information to Provide When Calling for Help
1. Farm location (upazila, district)
2. Number and type of birds (broiler/layer/Sonali, age)
3. When problem started
4. Number of sick birds and deaths
5. Main symptoms observed
6. Vaccination history
7. Recent feed changes
8. Any treatment already given
9. Whether nearby farms are also affected
